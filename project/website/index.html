The idea

Conflict is where moving habitats meet fixed human landscapes

As Europe's climate changes, the habitats species rely on are shifting across the continent, generally northward and toward higher elevations. Wildlife moves to follow these changing conditions, but people, roads, cities, and agricultural land remain largely fixed in place. Human-wildlife conflict emerges where those moving habitats intersect with human-dominated landscapes, through livestock losses, crop damage, vehicle collisions, and wildlife appearing in places where people are not used to seeing them.

Most existing models learn from historical conflict records to predict future conflict.

We take a different approach.

Rather than learning from past interactions, we model the processes that create them: habitat shifts, species movement, landscape connectivity, and the pathways animals use as they redistribute under climate change. Because our model is never trained on conflict data, its agreement with observed conflict patterns represents a genuine prediction rather than a rediscovery of the data it was given.

01

A foundation built on habitat models we trust

To understand where species may move in the future, we first needed to understand where they can live today. Using 57,838 GBIF observations, we built habitat models for seven European mammal species, using a maximum entropy approach implemented through its mathematically equivalent penalized Poisson point process formulation, and used them to map suitable habitat under both present and future climates.

Figure 1 — Framework & data. Occurrence density; realized climatic niches; the Gibbs→transport→topology pipeline; and spatial cross-validation skill.

Figure 1a - Georeferenced Records

We utilize open-access GBIF sightings of seven mammal species across Europe. Because this is "presence-only" data (we know where species were spotted, but not where they weren't), we use specialized Poisson point process models designed to handle this inherent ecological bias.

Figure 1b – Climatic Niches

To map each species' environmental comfort zone, we condensed dozens of complex climate variables into two Principal Components (PCs) that capture nearly 80% of Europe's climatic variation. This visually separates highly adaptable climate generalists from climate specialists.

Figure 1c – Mechanistic Pipeline

This flowchart traces our entire modeling journey. By feeding raw occurrences and climate niches into physical movement algorithms, we generate our final Coexistence Risk Index without ever using historical conflict data for training—ensuring our predictions are truly mechanistic.

Figure 1d – Model Skill

Rather than using standard random validation-which can artificially inflate accuracy due to nearby, similar locations-we implemented strict spatial cross-validation. By hiding entire geographic blocks during testing, we prove our model's real-world predictive power.

The geometry of a moving range

To predict future conflict, we first need to understand how habitats move, stay connected, and change shape as the climate changes.

This map compares where suitable habitat exists today with where it is expected to exist in the future. As the climate warms, many species lose habitat in southern Europe and gain habitat farther north or at higher elevations. This is the first step in understanding how wildlife ranges change over time.

This map shows the predicted direction of habitat movement under climate change. The arrows reveal a general shift from southern Europe toward cooler northern and higher-elevation regions where future conditions are expected to remain suitable.

This graph summarizes the movement shown in the map above. It shows that habitat shifts are strongest across central and northern Europe, while southern habitats retreat. Together, these patterns reveal a clear northward movement driven by climate change.

This map combines habitat movement with human activity. Yellow and orange regions highlight places where climate-driven habitat shifts overlap with areas of high human activity, making future human–wildlife conflict more likely.

From geometry to prediction

Each figure reveals a different piece of the puzzle. Some show how habitats shift as the climate changes, others identify the safest routes for wildlife movement, and others measure how habitat networks become fragmented. Together, they provide a more complete picture of how landscapes change over time.

Looking at all of these patterns together allows us to predict where people and wildlife are most likely to share space in the future. Rather than reacting to conflict after it occurs, these methods help us anticipate where conservation efforts will be needed most.

1

The habitat models are accurate validated

Across 37,070 land cells and 57,838 records, the from-scratch MaxEnt/PPP models reach spatial-block cross-validation AUC of 0.76–0.92 (mean 0.84) and Boyce indices up to 0.99. Skill tracks ecology: the climate-restricted golden jackal is most predictable; the ubiquitous red fox least. This is the trustworthy foundation everything else stands on.

2

Climate forces a coherent poleward push

Under +2.8 °C (SSP2-4.5, 2081–2100), the optimal-transport map assigns every taxon a northward displacement — a community-mean Wasserstein shift of 484 km (per-species 364–615 km). Displacement peaks at mid-latitudes and along retreating southern margins. This is the "animal movement" the rest of the pipeline must route.

3

Movement funnels into conflict corridors, and the range frays

Routing that demand through the landscape's resistance concentrates it into discrete conflict corridors and pinch-points — the Alps, the Poland–Belarus lowlands, and farmland–forest edges. Persistent homology shows the range's interior gaps (H1 loops) rising from 2,389 to 2,536: more fragmentation means more edge, and edges are where people and wildlife meet.

4

Two independent tests — neither uses conflict data out-of-sample

Test 1 (mechanism). Splitting each species' records into a 1990–2007 and a 2015–2026 epoch, five of seven taxa shifted poleward exactly as predicted; predicted and observed shifts are rank-correlated (Spearman ρ = 0.64). The grey wolf is the lone outlier — its recent range change is driven by legal-protection recolonization, a known non-climate confound.
Test 2 (interface). Coexistence Friction significantly predicts which human-dominated cells actually hold recent wildlife records (permutation p = 0.0005, 4,618 cells) — despite never being trained on those labels.

5

The result is robust, and the solver is provably correct

Under harsher warming (SSP5-8.5) the shifts grow (mean 714 km vs 484) but the spatial pattern of conflict barely moves (r = 0.98). The transport distance is grid-invariant (CV 0.5%), and the from-scratch solver reproduces the defining maximum-entropy property to r = 0.99998.

An honest caveat — where the map can mislead

Not every bright cell is a real hotspot. Coastal and island cells pick up artificial friction: because the optimal-transport step must move their habitat mass across open water, a large displacement crosses the human-pressure gradient even when the habitat itself barely changes. Scotland is the clearest case — its friction is the highest anywhere, yet its actual habitat gain or loss is minimal (Figure 2a). The north-eastern Baltic / Gulf-of-Finland corner is bright for a similar coastal reason layered on genuinely high human pressure. The most robust hotspots — high friction backed by real habitat change — are the Alps and the Poland–Belarus lowland belt.

Significance

Why this matters

Conservation budgets and human safety both depend on knowing where trouble will appear before it does. Today's conflict maps are retrospective — they need a history of clashes to learn from, which most regions and most species simply don't have.

Our framework needs none. It forecasts emergent conflict from physically-grounded principles — the transport, connectivity, and topology of a warming-driven range shift — so it can be applied to data-poor species and un-surveyed regions, and it produces an interpretable, falsifiable mechanism instead of a black box. Because the geometric layer sits on top of any suitability model, it upgrades the thousands of existing MaxEnt studies rather than replacing them.

In one sentence

You can forecast where human–wildlife conflict will emerge purely from the geometry of how climate forces habitat to move — turning a scarce-data problem into a solvable geometry problem.

Conclusions

The takeaway

We reframed human–wildlife conflict forecasting as the geometry of moving measures. A from-scratch maximum-entropy model gives trustworthy habitat maps; optimal transport says how far and where they must move; spectral connectivity says whether the landscape lets them; and persistent homology says how fragmented — and edge-exposed — the range becomes. Fused, they predict conflict hotspots that match independent data the model never saw.

The whole pipeline runs on openly downloadable data with a single command, and every figure here is real, committed output.
